\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\para{Hallucination benchmarks and taxonomy.} The study of LLM hallucinations has produced several benchmarks and taxonomies. \citet{lin2021truthfulqa} introduced \truthfulqa, demonstrating that larger models can be less truthful due to \imitativefalsehoods---answers matching common misconceptions in training data. Recent surveys~\citep{huang2023survey, zhang2023siren} distinguish between factuality hallucinations (contradicting established facts) and faithfulness hallucinations (diverging from source content). \citet{li2023halueval} provide HaluEval for fine-grained hallucination evaluation. Unlike these works that treat hallucinations uniformly, we focus specifically on identifying which hallucinations transfer across model families.

\para{Hallucination detection methods.} Detection approaches fall into two categories. Black-box methods like SelfCheckGPT~\citep{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} leverage self-consistency: factual responses are consistent across multiple samples, while hallucinations diverge. White-box methods access internal model states. \citet{chen2024inside} propose EigenScore, using eigenvalues of sentence embedding covariance matrices to identify overconfident hallucinations. \citet{kossen2024semantic} train probes on semantic entropy signals. These methods assume hallucinations are detectable through inconsistency or uncertainty signals. Our work reveals that \naturalhall are both consistent and confident, potentially evading these detection methods.

\para{Self-recognition and correction.} \citet{zhang2023snowball} demonstrate that models can often recognize their own errors when asked separately, yet commit to them during generation---a phenomenon they call ``hallucination snowballing.'' They show ChatGPT identifies 67\% of its mistakes and GPT-4 identifies 87\%. Our findings complement this work: while models can recognize many errors in isolation, \naturalhall represent a subset where recognition rates drop to 24\%, indicating true blindspots rather than contextual commitment errors.

\para{Mitigation strategies.} Approaches to reducing hallucinations include retrieval-augmented generation~\citep{lewis2020retrieval}, chain-of-thought prompting~\citep{wei2022chain}, and training models to express uncertainty~\citep{zhang2023rtuning}. RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022training} has improved factuality but does not eliminate hallucinations. Our results suggest that mitigation strategies should distinguish between random hallucinations (addressable through existing methods) and \naturalhall (requiring systematic training data interventions).

\para{Cross-model analysis.} Prior work has not systematically studied which specific questions cause errors across diverse model families. The \truthfulqa paper notes that different model families show similar inverse scaling patterns, suggesting shared biases, but does not identify specific transferring errors. We provide the first systematic analysis of hallucination transfer, robustness, and temporal persistence across model generations.
