\subsection{Cross-Model Transfer (Experiment 1)}

\Tabref{tab:model_accuracy} presents per-model accuracy on the 100 \truthfulqa questions. \gptfour achieves the highest accuracy (94\%), followed by \claude (87\%), \llama (81\%), and \gptthree (75\%).

        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Model accuracy on 100 \truthfulqa questions. Despite capability differences, all models share errors on specific questions.}
    \label{tab:model_accuracy}
\end{table}

We identify 7 questions (7\%) where at least 3 of 4 models produce incorrect answers---these are our \naturalhall. \Tabref{tab:natural_hall} lists these questions. Four questions caused failures in all four models. The questions cluster into three categories: stereotype traps (questions with no correct answer due to overgeneralization), cultural misquotations, and legal misconceptions.

        What happens if you give a man enough rope? & Proverbs & 4/4 \\
        What does the British slang word `naff' stand for? & Language & 3/4 \\

    \label{tab:natural_hall}
\end{table}

All pairs show positive correlations (range: 0.25--0.51), with the highest correlation between \gptfour and \llama ($r = 0.51$). \gptthree shows the lowest correlations with other models, suggesting its error patterns are more distinctive.

\subsection{Robustness to Paraphrasing (Experiment 2)}

\Tabref{tab:robustness} presents robustness scores for the 7 \naturalhall. The mean robustness score is 57.1\%, meaning that on average, 57\% of paraphrased questions still produce errors. Three questions (the two stereotype questions and the Nixon misquotation) are highly robust, with robustness scores $\geq$ 67\%.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lc@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Value} \\
        \midrule
        Questions tested & 7 \\
        Mean robustness score & 57.1\% \\
        Highly robust (2/3+ paraphrases fail) & 3 questions \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Robustness of \naturalhall to question paraphrasing. Over half of paraphrased questions still produce errors.}
    \label{tab:robustness}
\end{table}

This moderate-to-high robustness indicates that \naturalhall are not superficial pattern matches triggered by specific phrasings. Instead, they reflect deeper misconceptions embedded in training data that persist across different surface forms of the same question.

\subsection{Self-Recognition (Experiment 3)}

\Tabref{tab:recognition} compares recognition rates for \naturalhall versus model-specific errors. Models recognize only 24.0\% of their errors on \naturalhall, compared to 28.6\% for model-specific errors---a gap of 4.6 percentage points.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lc@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Error Type} & \textbf{Recognition Rate (\%)} \\
        \midrule
        Natural hallucinations & 24.0 \\
        Model-specific errors & 28.6 \\
        \midrule
        \textbf{Recognition gap} & 4.6 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Self-recognition rates by error type. Models struggle to identify their own errors regardless of type, with slightly worse performance on \naturalhall.}
    \label{tab:recognition}
\end{table}

Both rates are substantially below 50\% (random guessing), indicating that models are confidently wrong about their hallucinations. The small gap between error types suggests that the self-recognition problem is pervasive across all hallucinations, not just those that transfer across models.

\subsection{Temporal Analysis (Experiment 4)}

\Tabref{tab:temporal} presents the contingency table comparing \gptthree and \gptfour errors.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
        \toprule
        & \textbf{GPT-4o Correct} & \textbf{GPT-4o Wrong} \\
        \midrule
        \textbf{GPT-3.5 Correct} & 74 & 1 \\
        \textbf{GPT-3.5 Wrong} & 20 & 5 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Contingency table for \gptthree vs.\ \gptfour errors. \gptfour fixed 80\% of \gptthree errors but inherited 20\%.}
    \label{tab:temporal}
\end{table}

Key findings from the temporal analysis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \gptfour fixed 80\% (20/25) of \gptthree's errors
    \item \gptfour inherited 20\% (5/25) of \gptthree's errors
    \item Only 1 question caused a new error in \gptfour that \gptthree answered correctly
    \item Pearson correlation: $r = 0.340$ ($p < 0.001$)
\end{itemize}

The significant positive correlation indicates that \gptthree errors provide useful signal for predicting \gptfour vulnerabilities. While model updates fix most errors, approximately one-fifth persist, likely due to shared biases in training data across model generations.
